Community Scam Awareness Event

A free educational session for older adults and caregivers

Older adults are increasingly targeted by phone, email, and online scams — and the tactics used by scammers are becoming more sophisticated every year. Many people feel unsure about what to do when something seems suspicious, or worry about making a mistake online.

This event is here to help. Our goal is to give you clear, practical knowledge so you can feel confident — not fearful — when navigating phone calls, emails, and the internet. You will leave with concrete steps you can take and people you can call.

About the Event

A Community Event, Supported by the University

This event is organized in partnership with a local university and community organizations. It is open to the public and completely free of charge.

Nothing is being sold here. There are no products, memberships, or services being promoted. This is a straightforward educational session — designed to be simple, supportive, and practical for people of all backgrounds and comfort levels with technology.

What You'll Learn

Practical Knowledge You Can Use Right Away

This session covers the topics that matter most — explained clearly, without technical jargon. Here is what will be covered:

  • Common phone, email, and online scams — what they look like and how they work
  • Warning signs to watch for before you respond to a call, click a link, or share information
  • What to do when something feels suspicious — step by step
  • How to safely contact your bank, a government agency, or a trusted institution directly
  • How to report a scam to the appropriate authorities
  • Tips and guidance specifically for family members and caregivers

No prior experience with technology is needed. Everything will be explained in simple, everyday language.

Resources

Simple Safety Reminders

These are a few of the most important things to keep in mind. We will go over all of these in detail at the event.

Before you respond, remember:

  • Never share a verification code — not with anyone, including someone claiming to be from your bank or a government agency
  • Pause when something feels urgent — scammers create pressure; legitimate organizations give you time
  • Hang up and call back — if unsure, hang up and call the organization directly using a number from their official website
  • No real agency asks for gift cards — if someone asks you to pay with a gift card, it is a scam
  • When in doubt, do nothing — it is always safe to wait, ask someone you trust, or call us

To report a scam: Contact the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov, or call your local police non-emergency line. You may also call AARP's Fraud Helpline at 1-877-908-3360. We will provide a printed resource sheet at the event.
